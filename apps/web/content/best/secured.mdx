faqs:
  - q: "How is a secured card different from a debit card?"
    a: "A debit card pulls money directly from your checking account. When you use it, you spend money you already have. Debit card activity does not appear on credit reports and does not build credit. A secured card is a line of credit backed by your deposit. When you use it, you are borrowing money even though the issuer holds your deposit. That borrowing and repayment activity gets reported to credit bureaus and builds your credit history. The deposit is not the money you spend; it is collateral."
  - q: "How long does it take to build credit with a secured card?"
    a: "Most people see meaningful score improvement within 6 to 12 months of consistent on-time payments and low utilization. Getting from no credit or poor credit to the 670 threshold that unlocks standard unsecured cards typically takes 12 to 18 months. Getting to 720 or above usually takes 2 years or more, depending on your starting point and whether you have negative items being aged off your report. There are no shortcuts. Consistent behavior over time is the only thing that works."
  - q: "Will I get my security deposit back?"
    a: "Yes, in two ways. If you graduate to an unsecured card (like Discover's automatic upgrade process), the issuer returns your deposit and converts your account to an unsecured line. If you close the account in good standing without graduating, the deposit is returned minus any outstanding balance. The deposit is not used to pay normal charges, it only comes into play if you default and cannot pay."
  - q: "What credit score do I need for a secured card?"
    a: "Secured cards are specifically designed for people with no credit history or damaged credit. Many secured cards, including the Discover it Secured, do not have a minimum score requirement and evaluate applicants primarily based on income and ability to pay. If your score is 0 (no file) or even in the 500s, you can typically qualify. The main disqualifiers are active bankruptcies or very recent serious delinquencies."
  - q: "Can I have multiple secured cards?"
    a: "You can, but it is usually not necessary or advisable when you are starting out. Each application creates a hard inquiry, which temporarily lowers your score. One secured card used well accomplishes everything two secured cards would. If you do have multiple cards, keep each one active with small purchases and pay in full to avoid utilization issues. More accounts do help your credit mix and available credit over time, but the incremental benefit of a second secured card is small compared to just using one card responsibly."
  - q: "Does Discover really graduate you automatically?"
    a: "Yes. Discover reviews secured card accounts starting around month 7 and evaluates whether your payment history and overall profile qualify for an unsecured card. If you qualify, they upgrade the account automatically, return your deposit, and your account continues with the same history intact. Not everyone qualifies at 7 months, and Discover continues reviewing periodically. The key behaviors are: no missed payments, low utilization, and no other serious derogatory marks."
  - q: "What is credit utilization and why does it matter?"
    a: "Credit utilization is how much of your available credit you are using at any given time. If your secured card has a $500 limit and you carry a $400 balance, your utilization is 80%, which will significantly hurt your score. Most credit experts recommend staying below 30%, and ideally below 10%, at statement close. With a $500 limit, that means keeping your balance under $150. If you need to spend more, pay down the card mid-cycle before the statement closes."
  - q: "Should I pay my secured card balance in full or make minimum payments?"
    a: "Pay in full every month, always. Minimum payments accrue interest, which costs you money and does not build credit any faster than paying in full. The credit bureaus only care that you paid on time, not how much you paid. Paying in full also keeps your utilization low, which is the second biggest scoring factor after payment history. There is no benefit to carrying a balance on a secured card."
  - q: "Can I increase my credit limit on a secured card?"
    a: "On most secured cards, yes, by adding to your deposit. If you deposited $200 for a $200 limit and want a $500 limit, you deposit an additional $300. A higher limit with the same spending amount lowers your utilization ratio, which helps your score. Some issuers will also grant small unsecured limit increases over time as you demonstrate responsible use, even before formal graduation."
  - q: "What should I do when I graduate to an unsecured card?"
    a: "Do not close your old secured account if you can keep it open with no annual fee. Closed accounts lose their positive history over time. Keep the account open, make a small purchase on it occasionally, and pay it off. Your oldest account age is a factor in your score, and losing an account you opened while building credit can actually lower your score temporarily. The goal is to layer new accounts on top of the secured card foundation, not replace it."
