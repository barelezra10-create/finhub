The credit-building argument for getting a card in college is strong. By the time you graduate with a four-year degree, you could have four years of on-time payment history on your credit report. That translates directly to a higher credit score, which affects your ability to rent an apartment, your car insurance rate, and eventually your mortgage interest rate. Starting at 22 with four years of clean history is worth roughly four times what starting at 22 with no history is.

Student cards are different from regular cards primarily in their approval criteria. They accept applicants with no credit history, often no income requirements beyond being enrolled in school, and sometimes lower credit limits as a practical constraint. In exchange, they offer lower rewards rates than premium cards, though some student cards now match their non-student counterparts.

Who should get a student card? Any college student who can commit to using the card only for purchases they can afford to pay off in full each month. The number one way students damage credit is by carrying a balance and missing payments. Used correctly, a student card is one of the best financial decisions you can make in your twenties."

faqs:
  - q: "Can I get a credit card as a college student with no income?"
    a: "You can report regular financial aid, scholarships, grants, or money from family as income on most student card applications. The CARD Act of 2009 requires issuers to verify that applicants under 21 have independent income or a co-signer. Many students qualify using aid disbursements or part-time job income. If you cannot qualify independently, you can be added as an authorized user on a parent's card to start building credit that way."
  - q: "What credit score do I need for a student card?"
    a: "Most student cards are designed for applicants with no credit history at all. Discover's student cards accept applicants with scores from 580 up, and many will approve applicants with no score yet. Capital One and Bank of America have similar accessibility. Having any existing credit history, even from authorized user status on a parent's card, helps but is not required."
  - q: "Should I get a secured card or a student card?"
    a: "A student card first, if you qualify. Student cards do not require a security deposit, making them preferable for applicants who qualify. If you apply for a student card and are denied, or if you have a damaged credit history from before college, a secured card like the Discover it Secured is the right fallback. Both accomplish the same credit-building goal; student cards just do it without tying up your cash in a deposit."
  - q: "How much should I spend on my student card each month?"
    a: "Keep your balance below 30% of your credit limit. If your limit is $1,000, that means carrying no more than $300 at statement close. Most credit experts recommend keeping utilization below 10% for optimal scoring. You do not need to spend a lot to build credit. Using the card for one small recurring subscription and paying it in full every month is enough to generate payment history."
  - q: "What happens to my student card when I graduate?"
    a: "Most student cards automatically convert to the standard adult version of the same card when you graduate and are no longer enrolled. Your account history stays intact, your credit limit may increase, and the rewards structure might change slightly. You do not need to apply for a new card. The long credit history from your student card becomes a foundation for your post-graduation credit profile."
  - q: "Is it bad to close my student card after getting a better card?"
    a: "Generally yes, at least in the short term. Closing a credit card removes its positive history from your available credit, which can raise your utilization ratio and eventually affect your average account age. The better approach is to keep the student card open with a small recurring charge (like a streaming service) and pay it automatically each month. This keeps the history active without requiring any attention."
  - q: "What is Discover's Cashback Match?"
    a: "At the end of your first 12 months as a Discover cardholder, Discover automatically doubles the total cashback you earned that year. If you earned $80 in cashback, Discover adds another $80. You do not need to do anything, no claim to file, no minimum threshold. This effectively doubles your first-year reward rate. The match applies to all cashback earned from day one through month 12."
  - q: "Can a student card hurt my credit score?"
    a: "Only if misused. Common ways student cards hurt credit: carrying high balances relative to the credit limit (high utilization), missing payments or paying late (the single biggest scoring factor), and applying for multiple cards at once (each application creates a hard inquiry). Used correctly, a student card builds positive payment history that helps your score substantially over time."
