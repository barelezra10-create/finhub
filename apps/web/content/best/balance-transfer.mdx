intro: "A balance transfer credit card does one thing better than almost anything else in personal finance: it pauses interest. When you transfer high-interest credit card debt to a card with a 0% intro APR, every payment you make goes entirely toward principal instead of partially disappearing into interest charges.

The math is stark. A $5,000 balance at 22% APR costs $91 per month in interest if you only pay the minimum. On a 0% balance transfer card, $91 per month pays down $1,092 in principal in a year. That is the difference between spinning your wheels and making real progress.

The mechanics are straightforward but have important details. You apply for a balance transfer card, are approved, then request the transfer. The new issuer pays off your old card directly. The transfer usually takes 7 to 14 days. There is typically a 3% to 5% transfer fee charged upfront, but even a 3% fee on $5,000 is $150, which you recoup in the first two months of no interest at a 22% rate.

Who should use a balance transfer card? Anyone carrying credit card debt at 18% APR or higher who has a realistic plan to pay the balance down within the promotional period. The key word is plan. If you transfer $5,000 and do not pay it down before the promo ends, you are back to high-interest debt, now on a different card. Balance transfers are a tool, not a solution."

faqs:
  - q: "How does a balance transfer actually work?"
    a: "You apply for a balance transfer card, are approved, and then contact the new issuer (or complete the request online) to initiate the transfer. You provide the account number and amount from the old card. The new issuer pays that card directly, and the balance moves to your new card within 7 to 14 days. You then owe the new card. The transfer fee (usually 3%) is added to your transferred balance or charged separately. Until the old card is paid and verified zero, keep monitoring it."
  - q: "What is the balance transfer fee?"
    a: "Most balance transfer cards charge 3% to 5% of the transferred amount. On $5,000, a 3% fee is $150. This is added to your new card balance. Even at 3%, the math almost always favors the transfer: at 22% APR, you pay $110 in interest per month on $5,000, so you recoup the $150 fee in less than two months. Compare the upfront fee to the monthly interest cost on your current card to verify the transfer makes sense."
  - q: "Can I transfer a balance from any card?"
    a: "You cannot transfer a balance between cards from the same issuer. You cannot transfer a Chase to another Chase card, or a Citi to another Citi card. The transfer must be between cards from different issuers. Also, you can only transfer up to your approved credit limit on the new card, minus any outstanding purchases."
  - q: "What happens when the 0% period ends?"
    a: "Any remaining balance starts accruing interest at the standard variable APR, which is typically 18% to 27%. If you have $2,000 remaining after the promo period at 22% APR, you will pay roughly $36.67 per month in interest. This is why having a payoff plan before you transfer is essential. Know your monthly payment needed to zero the balance before the promo ends and set up autopay for that amount."
  - q: "Does a balance transfer hurt my credit score?"
    a: "The application creates a hard inquiry, which temporarily reduces your score by a few points. Opening a new account can also temporarily lower your average account age. However, the transfer itself can improve your utilization ratio if the new card has a high limit. Over the medium term, consistently making on-time payments on the new card is the most important factor. Balance transfer cards are credit neutral to positive when used correctly."
  - q: "Can I make purchases on a balance transfer card?"
    a: "Yes, but be careful. Some cards apply the 0% promo rate to both purchases and transfers. Others apply it only to transferred balances, and new purchases may start accruing interest immediately. Check your card's terms. Even if purchases are covered by the promo, adding new spending to a balance transfer card while trying to pay off the transferred debt slows down your payoff. Best practice: use a different card for purchases while paying down the transfer balance."
  - q: "How much should I transfer?"
    a: "Transfer only what you can realistically pay off within the promotional period. Divide your target balance by the number of months in the promo period to find your required monthly payment. If you cannot afford that payment, either transfer less or choose a card with a longer promo period. Transferring more than you can pay off simply delays the problem."
  - q: "Is there a limit to how many balance transfers I can do?"
    a: "There is no industry-wide limit, but each transfer card has a credit limit, and you cannot transfer more than that limit. Also, repeatedly opening balance transfer cards to roll debt forward is a strategy with diminishing returns: it adds hard inquiries, lowers average account age, and eventually becomes harder to sustain as your credit profile shows the pattern. Balance transfer cards are a tool for a one-time paydown effort, not a perpetual interest avoidance strategy."

A balance transfer card does its best work for people who come to it with a plan. Calculate the monthly payment required to zero your balance before the promo period ends, set up autopay for that amount from day one, and stop adding to the balance on the old card.

The Citi Double Cash at 18 months is the longest standard offer available and becomes a top everyday cashback card once the debt is gone. If your balance is smaller or your timeline shorter, the Wells Fargo Active Cash or Chase Freedom Unlimited offer 15 months of 0% with strong ongoing rewards.

The transfer fee is real but small compared to the interest savings. Do the math on your specific balance and rate, then act.
