intro: "Bad credit is not permanent. A credit score in the 500s or below does not mean you are locked out of credit for life. It means you need the right tools and a specific strategy to rebuild, and that process takes 12 to 24 months of consistent behavior.

The mechanism for rebuilding credit is straightforward: payment history is 35% of your FICO score, the single largest factor. Adding positive on-time payments to your record each month gradually outweighs past negative marks. As negative items age (late payments lose most of their impact after two years and fall off after seven), and as you add positive history, scores recover.

Secured credit cards are the primary tool because they accept applicants regardless of credit history. You deposit cash upfront as collateral, get a credit line equal to your deposit, use the card responsibly, and the activity reports to credit bureaus. The card does not look or function differently than a regular card when you use it at stores or online.

Credit-builder products like the Chime Credit Builder and Self Visa work differently but serve the same purpose for people who either cannot qualify for secured cards or who want a non-deposit path to building credit.

One rule applies to everything on this list: pay in full every month and keep your balance below 30% of your limit. Interest charges and high utilization will slow or reverse any progress you make on your score."

faqs:
  - q: "How quickly can I rebuild my credit score?"
    a: "The fastest documented improvements happen in the first 6 to 12 months of consistent on-time payments with low utilization. A score in the 500s can realistically reach the 620 to 640 range within 6 months if you have no other negative marks accumulating. Getting from 500 to 700 typically takes 18 to 36 months. The speed depends on your starting point, the severity of past negative marks, whether those marks are still accumulating, and how consistently you practice good habits with your new card."
  - q: "What credit score do I need for a secured card?"
    a: "Most secured cards on this list are designed for people with scores in the 300 to 579 range or no score at all. Discover's secured card has no published minimum score. Capital One Platinum Secured and OpenSky Secured accept applicants with very limited or damaged credit. The key disqualifiers for most secured cards are an active open bankruptcy or very recent serious delinquencies still accumulating. If you have discharged a bankruptcy, many secured cards will approve you."
  - q: "Will a secured card hurt my credit score?"
    a: "The application creates a hard inquiry (except OpenSky, which does no credit check), causing a small temporary score drop. But within a few months of on-time payments, the positive reporting outweighs the inquiry impact. Over time, a secured card is one of the most reliable tools for improving a credit score. The only ways it hurts your score are: missing payments, carrying a high balance relative to your limit, or closing the account prematurely."
  - q: "How much should I deposit on a secured card?"
    a: "Deposit enough to set a limit that lets you use the card for real purchases without hitting 30% utilization. If you deposit $300, your limit is $300, and you should keep your balance under $90 at statement close. A $500 deposit allows balances up to $150 without hurting your utilization score. A larger deposit gives you more room to use the card naturally while staying within good utilization range."
  - q: "Can I get my deposit back?"
    a: "Yes. For graduated cards like Discover it Secured and Capital One Platinum Secured, the deposit is returned when you qualify for the unsecured version and your account converts. For other cards, closing the account in good standing returns the deposit, minus any outstanding balance. Deposits are generally returned within 2 to 4 weeks after account closure or graduation. Do not factor the deposit into your monthly budget; treat it as locked savings until you graduate."
  - q: "Should I get a credit-builder loan or a secured card?"
    a: "A secured card is usually the more efficient credit-building tool because it adds revolving credit history, which is what most lenders and scoring models focus on. A credit-builder loan adds installment history, which is a different category. The Self Visa does both simultaneously, which is why it appears on this list. If you can afford a secured card deposit, start there. If you cannot come up with a deposit, a credit-builder loan through a credit union or Self is a viable alternative path."
  - q: "How do I know if I am ready to apply for an unsecured card?"
    a: "Most lenders targeting fair credit (cards like Citi Double Cash, Capital One Quicksilver) look for scores of 670 or above. If you have been using a secured card consistently for 12 to 18 months with no missed payments and low utilization, and your score has reached 650 to 670, you can start checking for pre-qualification offers without a hard inquiry. Most major issuers offer soft-pull pre-qualification tools."
  - q: "Does paying the full balance help my credit more than the minimum?"
    a: "For credit-building purposes, the bureaus only record whether you paid on time, not how much above the minimum you paid. However, paying in full avoids interest charges and keeps your balance low, which directly improves your utilization ratio. Both on-time payment history (35% of FICO) and utilization (30% of FICO) improve when you pay in full. The financial benefit of avoiding interest is also significant: a 25% APR on a $300 balance costs $75 per year if you carry it."

The Discover it Secured is the right starting card for most people rebuilding credit. The graduation path, real cashback rewards, and no annual fee make it the most complete offering in this category.

If you cannot qualify for Discover or need no credit check, OpenSky Secured provides guaranteed approval. If you bank with Chime, the Credit Builder card offers the most frictionless entry point.
