intro: "Dining is one of the most rewarded spending categories in the credit card industry. Cards routinely offer 3x to 4x points or cashback on restaurant purchases, which at standard redemption rates translates to an effective 3% to 6% return, well above the 1% to 2% you earn on most other spending.

The definition of dining on credit cards is broader than most people assume. Most issuers include restaurants, fast food, bars, cafes, food delivery apps, and sometimes grocery store food sections under the dining category. Check your card's terms: if Uber Eats, DoorDash, and Grubhub qualify (they do on many cards), the category earns on every delivery order, not just sit-down restaurant visits.

Who benefits most from a dining rewards card? Anyone who eats out or orders delivery more than twice per week. At $300 per month in dining spending, the difference between 1% and 4% earning is $9 per month, $108 per year, just from restaurant spending. Add a signup bonus and the first-year math is even more compelling.

One practical note: many of these cards earn their dining rewards only at restaurants and food delivery apps, not at grocery stores. A few cards blur the line, and some issuers have inconsistent category coding. If dining earnings are important to you, verify how your specific spending patterns are coded by running a small purchase through first."

faqs:
  - q: "What counts as a restaurant for credit card rewards?"
    a: "Most issuers code merchants in the MCC (Merchant Category Code) system. Restaurants, fast food chains, bars, cafes, and coffee shops typically fall under codes 5812 and 5814. Delivery apps like DoorDash, Uber Eats, and Grubhub often code as dining (5812) or food delivery. Grocery store delis and food courts may or may not qualify depending on the merchant's primary category code. If you are unsure whether a specific merchant counts, check your issuer's dining category definition or run a small test transaction."
